---
title: 'The Substrate Shift'
summary: "We're moving from silicon to biological computing, photonic chips, and DNA storage"
date: '2024-08-01'
---

For seventy years, the story of computing has been the story of silicon. The relentless, exponential march of Moore's Law, with its doubling of transistor density every two years, has been the engine of the digital revolution. It has given us everything from the supercomputer in your pocket to the global data centers that power the cloud. But that engine is starting to sputter. As we approach the fundamental physical limits of how small we can make silicon transistors, the easy gains are over. We are entering a new era, one defined not by the refinement of a single technology, but by a "substrate shift"—a diversification of the very physical materials on which we compute.

This isn't just about finding a faster replacement for silicon. It's about recognizing that different types of problems are best solved on different types of computational substrates. The future of computing is not a single, universal architecture, but a heterogeneous landscape of specialized hardware, including photonic chips, biological computers, and even DNA-based data storage.

The most immediate and commercially viable shift is toward photonics. Instead of pushing electrons through wires, photonic chips shuttle photons—particles of light—through tiny, on-chip waveguides. The advantage is speed and efficiency. Light travels faster than electrons and generates significantly less heat. This is a huge deal for data centers, where energy consumption and cooling are now the primary limiting factors. A significant portion of the energy used in a modern data center is not for computation itself, but for moving data around—from memory to processor, and between servers. Photonic interconnects can do this with a fraction of the power.

Companies like Ayar Labs and Lightmatter are already building chips that integrate photonic and electronic components. They use silicon for the logic gates but light for the communication. This hybrid approach is a pragmatic bridge to the future. It could lead to a massive increase in the performance of AI models, which are often bottlenecked by the speed at which they can access the vast amounts of data they need for training. A large language model's performance is as much about memory bandwidth as it is about raw processing power. Photonics breaks that bandwidth barrier.

Beyond just communication, researchers are also building fully photonic processors. These chips perform computations directly with light. By using different wavelengths of light to represent different data streams, they can perform certain types of calculations, particularly the matrix multiplications that are at the heart of neural networks, in a massively parallel way. This could lead to AI accelerators that are orders of magnitude faster and more efficient than the GPUs we use today.

An even more radical shift is the turn toward biological computing. This involves harnessing the computational machinery that has been perfected by evolution over billions of years. The most well-known example is DNA computing. A single gram of DNA can theoretically store over 200 exabytes of data—more than all the digital information created by humanity in a year. It's a storage medium of almost unimaginable density and durability. Researchers have already successfully encoded and retrieved text, images, and audio from synthetic DNA strands. This isn't for your family photos; it's for archival storage of humanity's most important information, a "seed bank" for the digital age.

But DNA isn't just for storage. It can also compute. In 1994, Leonard Adleman demonstrated that he could solve a simple version of the "traveling salesman problem" using DNA strands in a test tube. By encoding the cities and pathways as DNA sequences and using biochemical reactions to explore the possible routes, he showed that a massively parallel search could be performed at the molecular level. This approach is not well-suited for the kind of logic-based tasks that our current computers excel at, but it could be revolutionary for optimization problems, drug discovery, and materials science.

Beyond DNA, scientists are exploring the use of other biological systems. "Organoid intelligence" involves using lab-grown clusters of brain cells, or "brainoids," as a computational substrate. A recent study showed that a small brainoid could learn to play a version of the video game Pong. The neurons in the dish spontaneously organized themselves and learned to respond to the game's stimuli. This sounds like science fiction, but it points to a future where we could co-opt the learning and pattern-recognition abilities of biological neurons for certain types of tasks. This wouldn't be a "conscious" computer in the Hollywood sense. It would be a specialized, living processor that is exceptionally good at finding patterns in noisy, real-world data—something that our rigid, digital computers still struggle with.

This substrate shift is not a simple replacement of one technology with another. It's an expansion of our computational toolkit. A silicon-based CPU is a universal Turing machine, capable of solving any computable problem (given enough time and memory). It is a generalist. The new substrates are specialists. A photonic processor is a specialist in high-speed linear algebra. A DNA computer is a specialist in parallel search and high-density storage. An organoid computer could be a specialist in fuzzy pattern recognition.

The future of computing will be a hybrid, heterogeneous system where different tasks are routed to the most appropriate substrate. A request to a future AI assistant might be processed in a complex dance across multiple materials. The initial query might be transcribed from voice to text on a low-power neuromorphic chip that mimics the structure of the human ear. The core language processing might happen on a massive, photonically-interconnected cluster in the cloud. A related database query might be processed using an FHE-enabled chip to preserve privacy. If the query involves a complex optimization problem, it might be dispatched to a specialized DNA-based co-processor. The final result might be stored for long-term archival in a DNA data bank.

This is a far more complex, but also far more powerful and efficient, model of computation. It mirrors the structure of our own brains, which are not a single, monolithic processor, but a collection of specialized regions that have evolved to handle different tasks—vision, language, motor control, etc.

The challenge, of course, is immense. We need to develop the programming models, compilers, and interfaces to manage this new, heterogeneous hardware landscape. We need to figure out how to manufacture these new substrates at scale and how to integrate them into our existing infrastructure. And we need to grapple with the profound ethical questions raised by technologies like organoid intelligence.

But the shift is already underway, driven by the inescapable physics of silicon's decline and the insatiable demand for more computational power, particularly from the world of AI. The end of Moore's Law is not the end of progress. It is the end of the beginning. We are moving out of the silicon monoculture and into a rich, diverse, and fascinating new ecosystem of computation. The substrate is shifting, and it will change everything.
