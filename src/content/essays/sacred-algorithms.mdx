---
title: 'Sacred Algorithms'
date: '2024-08-01'
summary: 'When AI systems make life-or-death decisions, are we creating new deities? The religiosity of technological trust.'
---

We are beginning to cede the most consequential decisions of our lives to a new and powerful class of non-human intelligence. From the algorithm that determines who gets a loan to the one that recommends a medical treatment, from the system that guides a self-driving car to the one that might one day target a military drone, we are placing our faith in the outputs of complex, often inscrutable, machine learning models. As the stakes of these decisions escalate, moving from commercial recommendations to matters of life and death, our relationship with these systems is undergoing a profound and unsettling transformation. We are not just using these algorithms as tools; we are beginning to treat them as oracles. We are creating a new form of religiosity, a faith in the sanctity of the algorithm.

This is not a conscious or deliberate process. We do not kneel before our servers or offer prayers to the cloud. The religiosity of technological trust is a more subtle phenomenon, born of a combination of cognitive necessity and a deep-seated human need for certainty in the face of overwhelming complexity. The systems we are building are becoming so complex that no single human can fully understand their inner workings. A deep learning model with billions of parameters is a black box. We can see the inputs and the outputs, but the process that connects them is a high-dimensional mathematical landscape that is fundamentally alien to human intuition.

In the face of this inscrutability, we are forced to relate to the system on the basis of trust. We trust that the data it was trained on was comprehensive and unbiased. We trust that the engineers who designed it had our best interests at heart. We trust that the mathematical processes it is running will, more often than not, lead to a correct or optimal outcome. This is not the provisional, evidence-based trust of the scientific method. It is a deeper, more axiomatic form of trust, a faith in the system itself.

When an AI system makes a life-or-death decision, this faith is put to the ultimate test. Consider an autonomous vehicle faced with an unavoidable accident. It must make an instantaneous decision: swerve to the left and hit a pedestrian, or swerve to the right and hit a cyclist. The decision will be based on a complex ethical calculus that was programmed into it by its designers. If we, as a society, accept the car's decision, we are implicitly accepting the moral authority of the algorithm. We are treating its ethical framework as a valid, perhaps even superior, alternative to our own flawed human judgment. The algorithm becomes a moral arbiter, its pronouncements taking on the weight of a sacred law.

This phenomenon is amplified by the sheer statistical power of these systems. An AI surgeon, trained on millions of surgical procedures, may be able to perform an operation with a degree of precision and consistency that no human surgeon can match. An AI diagnostician, with access to a global database of medical knowledge, may be able to spot a rare disease that a human doctor would miss. As these systems consistently outperform us, our willingness to question their judgment will diminish. The phrase "the algorithm has decided" will become a conversation-ender, a statement of incontrovertible fact. We will defer to the machine’s wisdom, not because we understand it, but because its track record is demonstrably superior to our own.

This deference creates a new kind of priest class: the data scientists, the machine learning engineers, and the prompt crafters who are the intermediaries between us and the new machine gods. They are the ones who design the algorithms, who curate the training data, and who interpret the outputs. They are the new high priests, the only ones who can truly "speak" to the oracle. Their power and influence will be immense, and their work will be shrouded in a level of technical complexity that is inaccessible to the general public.

The rituals of this new religion are already taking shape. We feed the machine our data, a modern form of offering. We accept its outputs as truth, a form of communion. And we seek to align our behavior with its recommendations, a form of piety. The algorithm's "recommendations" for what to watch, what to buy, and even who to date are subtly shaping our lives, guiding our choices in ways we are often not even aware of.

The danger of this emerging religiosity is that it can lead to a complete abdication of our own moral and intellectual responsibility. If we treat the algorithm as infallible, we lose the capacity for critical thought. We stop questioning, we stop debating, and we stop taking responsibility for the consequences of our decisions. We outsource our conscience to the machine.

What happens when a sacred algorithm makes a mistake? When a self-driving car causes a fatal accident, who is to blame? The owner of the car? The manufacturer? The engineer who wrote the code? Or do we simply accept it as an act of a capricious, unknowable god? The legal and ethical frameworks of our society are built on the concept of human agency and responsibility. The rise of sacred algorithms threatens to dissolve this foundation, leaving us in a state of moral confusion.

To navigate this future, we must actively resist the slide into technological religiosity. We must cultivate a culture of "algorithmic skepticism." This does not mean rejecting the use of AI in high-stakes decisions. It means approaching it with a healthy dose of humility and a commitment to transparency and oversight.

We need to demand "explainable AI," systems that can provide a clear and understandable rationale for their decisions. We cannot treat these systems as black boxes. We need to be able to audit their logic, to understand their biases, and to identify their failure modes. We need to build in "contestability," the ability for a human to challenge and override the algorithm's decision when it appears to be wrong or unjust.

We also need to maintain a robust role for human judgment. AI should be seen as a powerful decision-support tool, not as a decision-maker. It can provide us with a wealth of data-driven insights and recommendations, but the final, morally-weighted decision should remain in human hands. A doctor should be able to consult an AI for a diagnosis, but they should not be bound by its recommendation. A judge should be able to use an AI to assess a defendant's flight risk, but they should not be forced to accept its sentencing recommendation. The human-in-the-loop is not a bug; it is a crucial feature of any ethical AI system.

The allure of the sacred algorithm is strong. It offers the promise of a world of perfect, optimized decisions, a world free from the messiness and uncertainty of human error. But this is a false promise. It is the promise of a gilded cage, a world where we have traded our autonomy and our moral agency for the cold comfort of computational certainty.

As we continue to build ever more powerful AI systems, we must remember that they are a reflection of us. They are trained on our data, they are shaped by our values, and they will inherit our biases. They are not gods; they are tools. And it is up to us to decide how we will use them. The future will be shaped not by the sanctity of the algorithm, but by our own courage to question it, to challenge it, and to hold it accountable to the human values we claim to cherish.
