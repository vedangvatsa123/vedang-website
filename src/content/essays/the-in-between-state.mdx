---
title: "The In-Between State"
summary: "Exploring transhumanism's awkward adolescence: the decades where we're enhanced but not transformed, stuck between human and post-human."
date: "2024-08-01"
---

The story of transhumanism is usually told in two acts. Act One is our current, un-enhanced state: fragile, mortal, brilliant but limited. Act Two is the post-human future: god-like beings with indefinite lifespans, vastly superior intelligence, and complete control over their own biology. It's a clean, dramatic leap. But this narrative skips the most important and most difficult part of the story: the long, awkward, and messy period in between. We are now entering this "In-Between State," transhumanism's adolescence. This is the era where we are enhanced but not yet transformed, patched-up but not redesigned, stuck in a liminal space between our biological origins and our technological destiny. And this period, which could last for decades or even centuries, will be defined by a new set of social, psychological, and philosophical challenges.

The In-Between State is a world of partial upgrades. It's not about replacing our brains with computers, but about implanting a chip that improves our memory or allows us to access the internet with a thought. It's not about achieving immortality, but about therapies that can reverse some aspects of aging, adding a few decades to our lifespan. It's not about designer babies, but about genetic interventions that can eliminate certain diseases or slightly enhance cognitive function.

This patchwork of enhancements will create a society of "glitchy" humans. Our bodies and minds will be a hybrid of old and new systems, and these systems won't always work together perfectly. Imagine a neural implant that gives you perfect recall, but is susceptible to being hacked. Imagine a gene therapy that strengthens your muscles, but has a rare side effect of causing an aggressive form of cancer twenty years later. The In-Between State is a beta test, and we are the testers. The "blue screen of death" will no longer be confined to our computers; it will be a potential experience for our own consciousness.

This will create a new and deeply personal form of inequality. In the past, the gap between the rich and the poor was one of wealth, of access to resources and opportunities. The In-Between State will introduce a biological gap. The wealthy won't just have better houses and faster cars; they will have better brains, stronger bodies, and longer lives. The first generation of truly effective anti-aging therapies or cognitive enhancements will be fantastically expensive. This will create a "biological stratification," a society where the elite are not just metaphorically, but literally, a different kind of human.

How does a society function when one segment of the population can expect to live to 150 with enhanced cognition, while another is still subject to the "natural" human lifespan of 80 years? How do political and social structures cope with a ruling class that might be in power for a century or more? The resentment and social friction would be immense. We would see the rise of "bio-political" movements, with "naturals" protesting against the "enhanced," and the enhanced perhaps viewing the un-enhanced with a mixture of pity and contempt.

The psychological challenges of this state will be just as profound. What is it like to live with a mind that is part biological, part digital? The experience of selfhood would be constantly in flux. An upgrade to your neural implant's operating system could subtly change your personality, your memories, your desires. Who are you, then? Are you the original biological consciousness, or the person you've become after the latest patch? The search for an "authentic self" would become a desperate and perhaps futile endeavor.

We would also have to grapple with the "upgrade treadmill." Just as we feel pressure to constantly upgrade our smartphones, we would feel pressure to upgrade our own bodies and minds. A new cognitive enhancement is released that offers a 10% boost in processing speed. Your colleagues are getting it. Can you afford not to? The In-Between State could be one of perpetual anxiety, of feeling that you are always one upgrade away from being obsolete.

Our social relationships would be strained. How does a "natural" human relate to an enhanced one? How does a couple navigate a relationship when one partner is aging and the other is not? How do parents decide which, if any, enhancements to give their children, knowing that their choices will shape not just their child's future, but their very biology? These are not just technological questions; they are deeply human ones, and we don't have the ethical or emotional frameworks to answer them.

The In-Between State is also an aesthetic and cultural minefield. What does beauty mean in a world of partial enhancements? We might see bizarre and experimental forms of body modification, a new kind of punk rock that uses biotech instead of safety pins. We would be living in a world of uncanny valleys, where some people look "almost" post-human, but not quite. The boundaries between the natural and the artificial would be a source of constant negotiation and anxiety.

Perhaps the most challenging aspect of this era is that it lacks a clear destination. The promise of the post-human is a distant, abstract one. The reality of the In-Between State is one of compromise, of imperfection, of living with the messy consequences of our own ingenuity. We will be pioneers in a new and unfamiliar territory, but without a map. We will be making up the rules as we go along.

This period will force a fundamental re-evaluation of what it means to be human. Is humanity defined by our biology, by our DNA? Or is it defined by our consciousness, our capacity for love, for creativity, for suffering? The In-Between State will put these two definitions in direct conflict. We will be forced to decide if a person with a synthetic heart, a genetically engineered immune system, and a neural-linked brain is still "human."

To navigate this period, we will need a new kind of wisdom. We will need to cultivate a sense of humility in the face of the complex systems we are tampering with. We will need a deep well of empathy to bridge the growing gap between the enhanced and the un-enhanced. And we will need a robust public discourse, a global conversation about the ethics and governance of human enhancement, to ensure that this new technology serves all of humanity, not just a privileged few.

The In-Between State is not the glorious future that transhumanist prophets have promised. It's the awkward, difficult, and dangerous transition. It's a journey through a landscape of unintended consequences, ethical dilemmas, and profound identity crises. We are not leaping into the future; we are wading into it, one partial, glitchy, and world-altering upgrade at a time. And how we navigate this messy middle will determine whether we arrive at a post-human utopia, or a fragmented, deeply unequal, and fundamentally broken version of ourselves.
