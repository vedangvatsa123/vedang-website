---
title: 'The Attention Refinery'
summary: 'How modern platforms have industrialized human attention extraction, and what post-attention economies might look like.'
date: '2024-08-01'
---

We've misunderstood the digital economy. For years, we’ve been told that data is the new oil. The metaphor is catchy, but it’s imprecise. Data, in its raw form, is abundant and inert. Oil has to be found, extracted, and refined before it has value. The real currency of the digital age isn't the data itself, but the resource required to give it value: human attention. This is the true crude oil, and the platforms we use every day have become ruthlessly efficient refineries, built to extract and monetize every last drop of it. This isn't just a business model; it's an industrial process that has fundamentally reshaped our cognitive and social landscapes.

The assembly line of this refinery begins not with a physical product, but with a psychological hook. The architects of our digital platforms, armed with insights from behavioral psychology, designed systems that don't just serve content but actively cultivate habits. The 'like' button, the infinite scroll, the pull-to-refresh mechanism—these weren't accidental features. They were precision-engineered tools designed to trigger dopamine loops, the same neural pathways associated with addiction. Nir Eyal’s “Hook Model”—Trigger, Action, Variable Reward, Investment—became the silent blueprint for a generation of products. The trigger is the notification on your phone. The action is opening the app. The variable reward is the unpredictable stream of social validation, outrage, or novelty you find there. The investment is your reply, your post, your data, which in turn primes the next trigger.

This cycle is the engine of the attention refinery. Each loop extracts a small amount of cognitive energy and time. In isolation, it's trivial. But aggregated across billions of users, performing these actions hundreds of times a day, it represents the largest and most systematic redirection of human consciousness in history. The output isn’t just user engagement; it's a predictable, quantifiable, and sellable inventory of human focus.

The industrialization is most apparent in the tools used. A/B testing, once a niche tool for optimizing website conversions, became a planetary-scale experiment in behavioral modification. Every tweak to a headline, every change in button color, every adjustment to the algorithm that sorts your feed is a micro-experiment designed to answer a single question: how can we capture a few more seconds of your time? This isn’t about making the product better for you. It’s about making you a better, more compliant resource for the system. The user is not the customer, nor even the product. The user is the raw material.

The consequences of this industrial process are now becoming terrifyingly clear. We're living through a cognitive load crisis, where the sheer volume of information overwhelms our capacity to process it. Our ability for deep, focused thought is eroding, replaced by a twitchy, reactive state of perpetual distraction. The algorithms that power these refineries don't optimize for truth, well-being, or social cohesion. They optimize for engagement. And it turns out that outrage, polarization, and misinformation are exceptionally engaging. The system, in its blind pursuit of efficiency, is refining away the very cognitive skills needed for a functioning democracy: critical thinking, nuanced debate, and shared reality.

What does it look like to move beyond this extractive model? A post-attention economy wouldn't seek to eliminate technology, but to realign it with human values. This requires a fundamental shift in how we measure success. Instead of optimizing for "time on site," what if we optimized for "time well spent"? This is not a fuzzy, feel-good metric. It’s a design principle with concrete implications.

Imagine a social network that, instead of an infinite scroll, presents a finite, curated selection of content and then actively encourages you to log off. Tristan Harris’s Center for Humane Technology has proposed concepts like this. A "calm technology" approach would prioritize tools that recede into the background, serving a function without demanding constant interaction. RSS readers, "read-it-later" apps like Pocket, and email newsletters represent early, user-centric attempts to escape the refinery. They put the user in control of consumption, breaking the algorithm's stranglehold on what we see and when we see it.

The economic model would also have to change. Subscription-based services offer one alternative. When users pay directly for a service, the incentive is to provide genuine value, not to hold their eyeballs hostage for advertisers. The success of platforms like Substack and Ghost shows a growing appetite for this model, at least among creators and their most dedicated followers. However, subscriptions can create their own echo chambers and accessibility issues. Not everyone can afford to pay for quality information, risking a future where the truth is a luxury good.

Another, more radical, possibility is the development of data unions or information co-ops. In this model, users would collectively own their data and negotiate its use with platforms. Instead of being the passive resource, users would become active stakeholders, receiving a share of the profits generated from their attention and data. Projects like Streamr and the Meeco platform are exploring this space, building the infrastructure for a more equitable data economy. This would represent a fundamental rebalancing of power, transforming the refinery into a partnership.

The technological underpinning for a post-attention economy is also emerging. Decentralized identity systems, built on principles of self-sovereign identity, would allow users to control their own data without relying on a central platform. You could grant specific, revocable access to your information, rather than signing it all away in a terms of service agreement you never read. Zero-knowledge proofs could allow you to prove things about yourself (e.g., that you are over 18) without revealing the underlying data (your date of birth). These tools don’t just offer privacy; they offer agency.

Building this future is not just a technical challenge. It's a social and political one. It requires digital literacy education that teaches people to recognize the architecture of manipulation. It requires regulatory frameworks that put the burden of proof on platforms to demonstrate they are not causing cognitive or social harm. The European Union's Digital Services Act and General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) are clumsy but necessary first steps in this direction. They begin to treat digital spaces as public squares with social responsibilities, not just private marketplaces.

The transition will be difficult. The attention refineries are deeply entrenched, with immense financial and political power. Their systems are designed to be compelling, and breaking free from them requires conscious effort. But the current model is unsustainable. A world optimized for distraction is a world that cannot solve complex problems like climate change, political polarization, or economic inequality. We are burning our most precious resource—our collective focus—to fuel a machine that produces little of lasting value.

The post-attention economy, therefore, is not a utopian dream. It is a necessary adaptation. It envisions a world where technology serves human goals, not the other way around. It’s a world where our tools help us think better, connect more deeply, and engage with the world more meaningfully. It’s a shift from a system that asks, "How can we get you to spend more time here?" to one that asks, "How can we help you live a better life, and then get out of your way?" This isn't about rejecting technology. It's about demanding that it respects our time, our minds, and our humanity. The refinery has been built. Now, we must decide if we want to continue being its crude oil or if we're ready to build something better.
