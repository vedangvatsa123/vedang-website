---
title: 'From Attention to Intention: The Next Evolution of the Web'
summary: 'The internet is transitioning from an economy based on capturing human attention to one centered on executing human intention through intelligent agents, fundamentally altering how we interact with the digital world.'
date: '2024-05-01'
---

For the past two decades, the internet has operated on a single, dominant business model: the attention economy. The core principle is simple. Human attention is a scarce and valuable resource. Platforms like Google, Facebook, and TikTok are not primarily in the business of search, social networking, or entertainment; they are in the business of harvesting and monetizing human attention. Their products are meticulously engineered to capture and hold your focus for as long as possible, so that it can be packaged and sold to advertisers. This model has shaped everything from user interface design to the very structure of online content.

We see its effects everywhere. Content is optimized for "engagement," which often means it's optimized for controversy, outrage, or triviality. News headlines become clickbait. Interfaces are filled with infinite scrolls and notification badges designed to trigger a dopamine response and keep you scrolling, watching, and clicking. In this paradigm, the user is not the customer; the user is the product.

But this model is reaching its limits, and a new paradigm is emerging: the intention economy. The fundamental shift is from capturing attention to executing intention.

What does this mean? In the attention economy, when you want to book a flight, you go to a website, you are shown a dozen options, you are presented with ads for hotels and rental cars, and you must manually navigate a complex interface to complete the task. The platform's goal is to keep you engaged, to expose you to as many monetization opportunities as possible.

In the intention economy, you simply state your goal: "I need to fly from New York to San Francisco next Tuesday, for the lowest possible price, on a flight that leaves after 5 PM." Your intention is then delegated to an autonomous or semi-autonomous agent. This agent, operating on your behalf, interfaces with the complex systems of airlines, aggregators, and booking platforms. It doesn't need a flashy user interface. It doesn't need to be "engaged." It simply needs to understand your goal and have the authority and capability to execute it. It finds the best flight, negotiates the price (perhaps with the airline's own agent), and completes the booking, presenting you only with the final confirmation. The human is no longer the operator; the human is the director.

This transition is being driven by the rapid advancement of Large Language Models. For the first time, we have machines that can understand natural human language with a high degree of nuance. We can express complex intentions to them, with constraints and preferences, and they can translate that intention into a series of actions.

This fundamentally alters the user's relationship with technology. The web ceases to be a place you "browse" and becomes a service you "direct." The primary interface is no longer a screen filled with links and buttons, but a conversation. You are not searching for information; you are tasking an agent to achieve an outcome.

The economic implications are immense. The value is no longer in capturing eyeballs but in successfully and efficiently executing a user's intent. A service that can book your travel itinerary with a single sentence is more valuable than a website that makes you click through ten pages of ads. The incentive for platforms shifts from maximizing time-on-site to minimizing time-to-completion.

This new economy will be built on different technical foundations. Instead of human-readable websites, the web will increasingly be composed of machine-readable APIs. Agents will communicate with other agents through structured data exchange, not by scraping HTML. Trust, which in the attention economy is a fuzzy concept of "brand safety," becomes a critical, verifiable attribute. You must be able to trust your agent to act in your best interest, to not leak your data, and to execute your instructions faithfully. This is where technologies like decentralized identity and verifiable credentials become not just useful, but essential infrastructure.

We are at the very beginning of this transition. The first glimmers can be seen in AI assistants and chatbots that can perform simple, multi-step tasks. But as these agents become more capable and are granted more autonomy, they will become the primary way we interact with the digital world. The web browser, the dominant tool of the attention economy, may become a relic. Why would you manually search for a product on a dozen different websites when you can simply tell your personal shopping agent what you want and have it find the best option for you?

This shift represents a profound re-alignment of technology with human interests. The attention economy, for all its success, has often created a parasitic relationship, where technology profits by distracting us. The intention economy promises a more symbiotic relationship, where technology profits by serving us, by taking our goals and making them a reality. It's a move from a web that consumes our time to a web that saves our time. It is the next logical step in the evolution of the internet.
