---
title: 'From Attention to Intention'
summary: "The internet is being rebuilt around digital agents that act on our behalf. This marks a fundamental transition from an economy based on capturing human attention to one built on serving human intention, a shift with profound consequences for how we interact with technology and how value is created."
date: '2024-07-22'
---

The foundational logic of the digital world is undergoing a profound structural transformation. For decades, the internet has operated as a colossal, decentralized apparatus for capturing and monetizing a single, finite resource: human attention. Its architecture, from search engine algorithms to social media feeds, is engineered to maximize engagement, pulling users into a vortex of content consumption. This model, however, is beginning to fracture under the weight of its own success and the rise of a new technological paradigm. We are moving from an internet where we are the product to an internet where our agents act on our behalf. This is the shift from an attention economy to an intention economy.

The attention economy is predicated on a simple, transactional loop. Platforms provide "free" content or services in exchange for the opportunity to sell our viewership to advertisers. The core metric is time on site, the scroll depth, the click-through rate. Every design choice, from infinite scroll to notification badges, is a carefully calibrated instrument to hold our focus for just a moment longer. In this model, the user is not the customer but the raw material. Our preferences, behaviors, and data are harvested, packaged, and sold to the highest bidder. The internet, in this form, is not a tool for us to use, but a system that uses us. Its primary function is not to help us achieve our goals but to distract us from them long enough to serve an ad.

This architecture has produced immense wealth and innovation, but it has also generated significant externalities. The relentless competition for attention has led to a degradation of the information ecosystem, prioritizing sensationalism over substance and outrage over nuance. It has created incentive structures that reward the spread of misinformation and has fostered business models that are inherently misaligned with user well-being. The system is designed to be addictive, to exploit psychological vulnerabilities to keep us hooked. The result is a digital environment that often feels noisy, overwhelming, and counterproductive.

The emergence of sophisticated AI agents, however, presents a fundamental challenge to this status quo. These agents, capable of understanding complex, long-term goals and executing multi-step plans to achieve them, introduce a new layer of abstraction between the user and the digital world. Instead of manually navigating a dozen websites to book a vacation, comparing flights, hotels, and rental cars, a user can simply state their intention: "Plan a seven-day trip to Kyoto in the spring for two people, optimizing for cultural experiences and staying within a $5,000 budget."

The agent then takes over. It doesn't just search; it evaluates, negotiates, and transacts. It can interface with the APIs of airlines, hotel chains, and local tour operators. It can read reviews, analyze travel times, and even book restaurant reservations. It operates not by capturing attention but by fulfilling intention. The user is no longer required to wade through a sea of ads and sponsored content. Their agent acts as a fiduciary, a digital representative whose sole purpose is to achieve the user's desired outcome in the most efficient way possible.

This shift from a "pull" model, where users are pulled into platforms, to a "push" model, where agents push a user's intentions out into the world, will reorder the digital economy. The value proposition is no longer about how many eyeballs you can capture, but how effectively you can serve an agent's request. The key interface ceases to be the graphical user interface (GUI) of a website or app and becomes the application programming interface (API).

In the agentic web, a business's success will depend on the quality and accessibility of its API. An airline that provides a clean, well-documented, and powerful API will be favored by travel agents over one that does not. A retailer with a robust API that allows for seamless, automated purchasing will win the business of personal shopping agents. The competitive advantage will shift from user experience (UX) design to API design. The new gatekeepers will not be search engines or social media platforms, but the agent ecosystems themselves.

This creates a new set of economic incentives. In the attention economy, the goal is to make tasks take longer, to introduce more steps, more pages, more opportunities to serve an ad. In the intention economy, the goal is efficiency. An agent is judged by how quickly and accurately it can fulfill its user's request. The business models will therefore shift from advertising to service fees, subscriptions, or commissions on transactions. Value will be captured by providing a superior service to the agent, not by distracting its human principal.

Consider the implications for search. Google's dominance is built on its ability to be the primary entry point to the internet, the arbiter of information. In an agentic web, a user's query is no longer a search term to be matched with indexed pages, but an instruction to be executed. The agent doesn't just return a list of links; it synthesizes information, performs actions, and delivers a result. The search engine as we know it becomes a backend service, one of many tools an agent might use to gather information. Its role is diminished, its power as a gatekeeper eroded.

This transition also has profound implications for privacy and data ownership. In the attention economy, our data is the currency. We trade it, often unknowingly, for access to services. In the intention economy, the agent acts as a custodian of our data. It can be designed to reveal only the minimum amount of information necessary to complete a task. When booking a flight, the agent only needs to provide the airline with the passenger's name and payment information, not their entire browsing history. This creates the potential for a more private, secure digital existence, where users have greater control over their personal information.

Of course, this vision of an agentic web is not without its challenges. The development of truly capable, trustworthy agents is a significant technical hurdle. These systems must be robust, secure, and aligned with human values. The potential for malicious agents, or for agents to be exploited, is a serious concern. The concentration of power in the hands of a few companies that control the dominant agent ecosystems is another risk. If a single corporation owns the primary interface through which we all interact with the digital world, it could become a gatekeeper of unprecedented power.

Furthermore, the transition will be messy. The incumbent giants of the attention economy will not cede their position without a fight. They will attempt to build their own agentic systems, to integrate agents into their existing platforms in a way that preserves their business models. We will likely see a hybrid period, where attention-based and intention-based systems coexist and compete.

Nevertheless, the underlying logic is compelling. The intention economy represents a more mature, more aligned vision of the internet. It is a vision where technology serves human goals, not the other way around. It promises a future where our digital tools are not sources of distraction but enablers of action, where our interactions with the internet are not a drain on our time but an extension of our will.

The construction of this new paradigm is already underway. The rise of large language models and the proliferation of APIs are the foundational layers of the agentic web. As these technologies mature, the shift from attention to intention will accelerate. It will not be a sudden revolution, but a gradual, inexorable transition. One by one, the pillars of the attention economy will be replaced by the services of the intention economy. The internet is being rebuilt, and when the dust settles, it will be a very different place. It will be an internet designed not to capture our attention, but to empower our intentions. The user will no longer be the product, but the principal. And that will change everything.
