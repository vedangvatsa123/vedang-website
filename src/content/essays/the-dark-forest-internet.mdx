---
title: 'The Dark Forest Internet'
summary: "As bots outnumber humans online, exploring the shift toward private, cryptographically verified communication channels and the death of the open web."
date: '2024-08-01'
---

The open web is dying. For years, we’ve operated under the assumption that the internet is a public square, a place for open dialogue and the free exchange of ideas. This was a beautiful, optimistic vision, but it was based on a flawed premise: that the participants would be, for the most part, human. That premise is no longer true. The internet is becoming a dark forest, and our behavior is starting to adapt.

The term "dark forest" comes from Liu Cixin's science fiction novel, "The Dark Forest." In the book, the universe is a dangerous place. Any civilization that reveals its location risks immediate annihilation by other, more advanced civilizations. The only rational survival strategy is to stay silent and hidden. On the internet, the predators aren't hostile aliens; they are far more mundane, yet just as destructive: swarms of automated bots, algorithmically-driven content mills, and bad-faith actors. They have made the open web an increasingly hostile environment for authentic human interaction.

The scale of the problem is staggering. Estimates vary, but it's widely accepted that bot traffic now exceeds human traffic online. These aren't just benign web crawlers indexing pages for search engines. They are scrapers stealing content, spammers flooding comment sections, and increasingly sophisticated AI agents designed to mimic human conversation, manipulate public opinion, and sow discord. Every open forum, every public comment section, every social media platform is a hunting ground. To post publicly is to expose yourself to this automated horde. Your words will be scraped, decontextualized, fed into language models, and used to train the very systems that are making the space uninhabitable.

This has led to a chilling effect, a phenomenon that tech commentator Yancey Strickler and others have labeled the "Dark Forest theory of the internet." As the open web becomes louder, more toxic, and more performative, real conversations are retreating into the shadows. People are moving from public feeds to private channels: group chats, Discord servers, Signal threads, and subscriber-only newsletters. These are the digital equivalents of campfires in the dark forest—small, illuminated spaces where trust can be established and genuine connection can occur.

The shift is visible everywhere. Once-vibrant blog comment sections are now either disabled or overrun with spam. Public Twitter (now X) has devolved into a chaotic arena of algorithmically amplified rage-bait and AI-generated replies. In response, thoughtful discourse has migrated to curated lists, private group chats, and subscriber-only "Notes" on platforms like Substack. The public-facing internet is becoming a stage for performative identity, brand building, and algorithmic manipulation, while the real human-to-human interaction happens elsewhere, out of sight.

This retreat is a rational response to a changed environment. In the dark forest of the internet, any public signal can be misinterpreted or weaponized. A nuanced opinion can be ripped from its context and used as fodder for an outrage mob. A personal story can be scraped and turned into clickbait. A creative work can be fed into an AI image generator without credit or compensation. Silence and obscurity become forms of self-preservation.

The next phase of this evolution is the move toward cryptographically verified communication. If you can't trust that the "person" you're talking to is real, you need a way to verify their identity and the authenticity of their message. This is where technologies like digital signatures and decentralized identity come in. Imagine a future where every message you receive is signed with a private key that proves it came from a specific, trusted individual. Your communication client could automatically filter out any unsigned or unverified messages, creating a secure channel free from the noise of the bot-infested public web.

Ethereum and other smart contract platforms provide a glimpse of what this might look like. Services like the Ethereum Name Service (ENS) allow users to link a human-readable name (like "vitalik.eth") to a cryptographic address. This creates a persistent, user-owned identity that is not tied to any single platform. You could use this identity to sign into a decentralized social network, publish a signed blog post, or participate in a verified group chat. Reputation becomes tied to a cryptographic key, making it much harder for bad actors to operate anonymously.

This leads to the concept of a "web of trust," a decentralized alternative to the platform-centric model. Instead of relying on Twitter or Facebook to verify identities and mediate conversations, you would rely on your own network of trusted contacts. You might subscribe to a friend's feed of signed messages. If they vouch for someone else by signing their public key, that person's messages might also appear in your feed, but perhaps with a different trust score. This is a return to a more human-scale model of social connection, but with a cryptographic backbone.

The implications of this shift are profound. The death of the open web as we knew it is not necessarily a tragedy. The "public square" was always a messy, often unpleasant, and increasingly fake place. Its replacement may be a more fragmented but also more meaningful digital world. We might lose the serendipity of stumbling across a brilliant stranger's blog, but we gain the security of knowing that the people we are interacting with are real and have something at stake.

This doesn't mean the public internet will disappear entirely. It will likely evolve into something else: a broadcast medium dominated by large corporations, media outlets, and a new class of "performative" individuals who are willing to brave the dark forest. It will be a place for marketing, entertainment, and mass communication, but not for genuine dialogue. It will be the glitzy, loud, and dangerous city center, while the real life of the community happens in the quiet, private suburbs.

The challenge will be to ensure that these new, private spaces don't simply become another form of echo chamber. The fragmentation of the web into countless private channels could exacerbate polarization, as people retreat into communities that only reinforce their existing beliefs. We need to design these new systems with an eye toward "connective tissue"—mechanisms that allow for discovery and interaction between trusted communities without exposing them to the dangers of the open forest. Perhaps this looks like shared, multi-signature "community feeds" or protocols for cross-server verified communication.

We are at an inflection point. The old dream of a single, open, global conversation is fading, washed away by a tide of automation and bad faith. The new reality is a "patchwork," as described by philosopher Samo Burja, a world of smaller, more resilient, and more coherent communities. The dark forest is a scary place, but it is also a place that forces us to be more intentional about who we connect with and why. By embracing the tools of cryptography and building smaller, more trusted spaces, we may be able to navigate it. It won't be the open web we were promised, but it might be an internet that is, once again, for humans. The future is not public; it's private, verified, and small. We must learn to build our campfires carefully.
