---
title: 'The Dark Forest Internet'
date: '2024-08-01'
summary: 'As bots outnumber humans online, exploring the shift toward private, cryptographically verified communication channels and the death of the open web.'
---

The open web is dying. The promise of a global village, a vibrant public square where ideas could be freely exchanged, has given way to a different reality. The digital spaces that were once bustling with human conversation are becoming eerily quiet, or worse, filled with the cacophony of non-human actors. We are entering the age of the Dark Forest Internet, a term borrowed from Liu Cixin’s science fiction trilogy, “The Three-Body Problem.” In the novels, the universe is a dark forest, teeming with hidden hunters. Any civilization that reveals its location risks immediate annihilation by a more advanced predator. The only rational survival strategy is to stay silent. A similar logic is beginning to take hold on the internet.

The predators in our digital forest are not alien warships, but a far more insidious force: an ever-growing army of bots, scrapers, and AI agents. These automated entities now account for the majority of internet traffic. They are scraping our data, mimicking our conversations, and polluting our information ecosystems with spam, propaganda, and synthetic content. The public spaces of the internet, the social media platforms, the comment sections, the open forums, are becoming hostile environments for genuine human interaction. Every word we post is a signal, a piece of data that can be harvested, analyzed, and used against us. The rational response, increasingly, is to retreat.

This retreat is not a complete withdrawal from the digital world. It is a strategic shift from the open, public web to a network of smaller, more secure, and more private spaces. We are seeing a mass migration from the public square to the digital equivalent of private clubs, secret societies, and encrypted channels. This includes everything from paid newsletters and subscription-based communities to private group chats on Signal and Telegram, and even more esoteric, cryptographically-gated social networks. In these spaces, identity can be verified, conversations can be protected, and the noise of the bot-infested public web can be filtered out.

The core technology enabling this shift is cryptography. Encryption, once a niche tool for spies and cypherpunks, is becoming a mainstream necessity for digital survival. End-to-end encrypted messaging apps provide a baseline level of security, ensuring that only the sender and the intended recipient can read the contents of a message. But the more profound change is the rise of cryptographic identity. Technologies like zero-knowledge proofs and digital signatures allow us to prove who we are and what we have access to without revealing unnecessary personal information.

Consider a decentralized social network built on these principles. To join a specific community, you don’t need to provide your email address or your real name. Instead, you might need to prove that you own a specific cryptographic token, a non-fungible token (NFT) that acts as a digital membership card. This allows for a form of pseudonymous but verified identity. The community knows you are a legitimate member, but they don’t need to know your real-world identity unless you choose to reveal it. This creates a space that is resistant to bots (which would need to acquire the token) and Sybil attacks (where one entity creates many fake accounts), while still allowing for a degree of privacy and freedom of expression.

The economics of the Dark Forest Internet are also fundamentally different from the surveillance-based model of the open web. The current model relies on extracting user data to sell targeted advertising. In the private, encrypted spaces of the dark forest, this model breaks down. The platform provider often cannot see the content of the users’ conversations, making it impossible to target ads. The business model, therefore, must shift from advertising to direct payment. Users pay a subscription fee for access to the community, or they purchase a token that grants them membership. This aligns the incentives of the platform with the users. The platform is no longer incentivized to maximize engagement at all costs; it is incentivized to provide a high-quality, secure, and valuable experience that people are willing to pay for.

This shift has profound implications for the future of information. The open web, for all its flaws, did create a shared context, a global conversation. The move to a fragmented landscape of private spaces could lead to a dramatic balkanization of discourse. We may retreat into our respective echo chambers, our beliefs reinforced by a community of like-minded individuals, with little exposure to differing points of view. The potential for a shared public truth could evaporate, replaced by a thousand competing, cryptographically-sealed realities. This is the great paradox of the Dark Forest Internet: in our quest for safety and authenticity, we may be sacrificing the very possibility of a common ground.

Furthermore, the death of the open web poses a significant threat to the discoverability of information. Search engines, the foundational tools for navigating the internet, are struggling to cope with the flood of AI-generated content. The signal-to-noise ratio is plummeting. As more valuable conversations move into private, encrypted spaces that are invisible to search engine crawlers, the public web risks becoming a shallow, polluted ocean of low-quality content. Finding reliable, human-generated information will become increasingly difficult. We may see the rise of a new kind of information broker, a human curator who has access to multiple private communities and whose value lies in their ability to synthesize and share insights from these hidden worlds.

The transition to a Dark Forest Internet is not an overnight event. It is a slow, gradual process of erosion. The public spaces will not disappear, but their nature will change. They will become more performative, more commercialized, and more heavily populated by non-human actors. They will be the brightly lit stage, while the real conversations, the authentic connections, happen in the shadows.

Navigating this new landscape will require a new set of skills. Digital literacy will no longer be about how to use a search engine, but about how to evaluate the trustworthiness of a private community. It will be about managing a portfolio of digital identities, some public, some pseudonymous, some anonymous. It will be about understanding the trade-offs between the reach of the open web and the security of the dark forest.

The rise of the Dark Forest Internet is not a future we should necessarily celebrate. It is a defensive adaptation to a hostile environment. It is a retreat from the grand, optimistic vision of the early web. But it may be a necessary one. The open web, in its current form, is becoming unsustainable. The relentless pressure of data extraction and algorithmic manipulation is turning it into a space that is increasingly inhospitable to the very thing it was designed to foster: genuine human connection. The move to smaller, more secure, and more intentional communities may be the only way to preserve it. We may be losing the global village, but in its place, we may be building a network of digital speakeasies, hidden gardens, and secret libraries. The forest is dark and full of hunters, but in the shadows, new forms of life are beginning to bloom.
