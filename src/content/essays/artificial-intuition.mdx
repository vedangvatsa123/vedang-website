---
title: "Building an Artificial Intuition"
summary: "We think of computers as purely logical machines. But what if we could teach them to have a 'gut feeling'?"
date: "2024-05-15"
---

We think of breakthroughs as flashes of lightning, moments of genius that defy logic. But intuition isn't magic. It's pattern recognition, supercharged. It’s the seasoned doctor who senses something is wrong before the test results are back, or the chess grandmaster who knows a move is right without consciously calculating every outcome. Their minds are running on a different kind of operating system, one built from years of experience and deep knowledge.

What if we could build something similar for machines?

The idea is to move beyond rigid, step-by-step processing. When a machine makes a decision, it’s usually because it followed a strict logical path. An artificial intuition would work differently. It would sift through vast, seemingly unrelated pools of information to find novel connections—the kind of sideways leaps that humans make. It's about giving the machine the ability to connect the dots in a way it wasn’t explicitly programmed to do.

To build this, you need to feed the machine different kinds of knowledge. First, there's the foundational, common-sense stuff, the things we all just know. Think of it as the machine's basic education, sourced from broad knowledge bases like Wikipedia. This is the existing, factual landscape.

Second, you need the messy, unstructured, and often opinionated information that reflects the public consciousness. Social media feeds, blogs, and forums are perfect for this. It's the digital equivalent of a "gut feeling" or the collective mood. This layer provides a kind of intuitive, general knowledge that’s often ahead of formal analysis.

Finally, you need deep, specialized expertise. This comes from academic papers, industry reports, and domain-specific texts. This is the contextualized knowledge, the kind an expert develops over a long career. It’s the detailed information that allows for nuanced understanding within a specific field, like business innovation or clinical diagnostics.

By weaving these three threads of knowledge together into a massive, interconnected network, you create a rich tapestry of information. The "intuitive" part comes from teaching the machine to find new, previously unseen paths through this network. When presented with a problem, it doesn't just look for a direct, logical answer. It explores the entire web of connections, looking for weaker, less obvious links that might lead to a truly innovative solution.

The goal isn't to replace rational thought, but to augment it. A system with artificial intuition could surface non-obvious relationships in data that a human analyst might miss. For instance, in business, it might connect a new technology with an unforeseen market need, not based on a direct market analysis, but on a web of subtle connections across technical papers, consumer trends, and expert commentary. It's about finding the "why" that isn't immediately apparent.

We're still a long way from creating a true artificial gut feeling. But by modeling how our own intuition works—combining broad knowledge, current trends, and deep expertise—we can build systems that don't just compute, but discover.
